About

What is a microfiber?

A microfiber is tiny fragment of the type of plastic used to make synthetic fabrics. These fibers are shed from synthetic clothing during a washing machine cycle. Because they are so small, microfibers aren’t all caught by wastewater treatment plants; instead, many are released into the environment.

How bad is the problem?

Right now, it’s estimated that fibers are, by count, the single largest contributor to watershed plastic pollution in developed countries and account for a significant portion of plastic waste entering the ocean. Though we don’t yet know the extent to which microplastic pollution might affect humans, we do know that many animals within the aquatic food chain ingest these plastics and the toxics they absorb from the water around them, which can transfer to their tissue and cause gut impaction, hormone disruption, and liver damage.

Which fabrics cause microfiber pollution?

Microfibers can be released from any synthetic fabric, including polyester, rayon, and acrylics, or blends of these materials with natural materials.

Unfortunately, most of these microfibers pass through our water filtration and make their way into the environment.

Solutions

Given the fact that synthetic microfibers are a major source of atmospheric pollution, it is important that we become aware of this problem and think about how we can develop solutions which involve changing our personal habits and advocating for societal change.

Here are a few very simple things you can do to reduce your impact!

Pay attention to the type of clothing you buy and focus on products that are made from natural fibers that do not produce microfibers.

Consider replacing your old washer with a front load washing machine to reduce the number of microfibers that end up in the ocean.

Consider helping an organization like Zero Waste Canada that is working on advocating for change to address microfiber pollution in our environment.
